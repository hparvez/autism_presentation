What is ASD?

Autism Criteria

Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction, including:
- Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
- Deficits in non-verbal communicative behaviours
- Deficits in developing, maintaining and understanding relationships

Restrictive, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests or activities, including:
- Repetitive motor movements, use of objects or speech
- Strict adherence to routines
- Very restricted, fixated interests that are high in intensity and focus
- Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory inputs

Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period, must cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational or other areas of functioning and can't be better explained by intellectual disability or developmental delay

ASD Stats

Autism Prevalence

Recent studies ( Source: "Global Prevalence of Autism: A Systematic Review Update, 2022") indicate that approximately 1% of the population are diagnosed with autism.

With that said, data across multiple studies indicate a material gender bias in favour of males. Currently, there's no scientific consensus as to why this might exist, aside from biases in diagnostic criteria - therefore, equalising for this bias suggests that autism prevalence may be as high as 4%.

We should note that, owing to advances in diagnostic capabiliies, and a greater undertanding and awareness of autism, prevalence has steadily increased over the past decades (some studies indicate a tripling of rates in the US between the years 2000 and 2020). Should these advances continue, the figures mentioned in this presentation likely underestimate the "true" prevalance.

Autism and IQ

Autism and intelligence (as measured purely by IQ in this instance) demonstrate a somewhat complex relationship.

By design, IQ tests normalise scores into a Gaussian distribution centered around a mean value of 100. Conversely, studies indicate that IQ scores of autistic individuals appear to conform to a bimodial distribution, with roughly equivalent twin peaks below 70 (considered cognitivitely impaired) and above 115 (considered very high intelligence) with considerably larger 'tails' at the extremes of the distribution than the typial population.

We note that, whilst large studies with statistically significant populations are difficult to find, it is estimated than 60% of autistic individuals demonstrate average or above average intelligence.

Source: "Autism Spectrum Disorder and IQ - A Complex Interplay", 2022

Unemployment

Despite the IQ figures shown on the previous slide, autistic individuals display the lowest employment rate of all disabilities recorded by the UK's Office for National Statistics (ONS) at 29%.

Further studies indicate that:

  • For those indiviuals with a college degree, the employment rate falls to only 15%
  • Those individuals with jobs appear to be materially underemployed, with "office and administrative support" and "food preparation and service" accounting for almost 40% of jobs
  • Approximately 80% of jobs held are part-time
  • Of those employed full-time, average earnings are a mere $8.08 per hour

    • (source: US Department of Education, NLST2)

What This Means ...

Begin with autistic 100 individuals

... 71 are unemployed

... Of the remaining 29, a further 23 are only employed on a part-time basis

... Of the remaining 6, a further 3 are underemployed (food service, admin support)

... This leaves only 3 individuals of the original 100 that are likely to be "fully" employed

... But wait! We know that 60 of these individuals are of "average" or "above average" intelligence!

... In fact, 40 of them are considered "highly intelligent"

Key takeaway: There's a significant, untapped, pool of highly inteligent autistic individuals that are not represented with the current workforce.

ASD-Friendly Workplace

Overview

The workplace can present a number of hurdles, even for neurotypicals - though for many autistic individuals, navigating this environment can feel we are running an entirely different race to the rest of the field. In this section, we'll briefly explore practical ways in which your company can alter its hiring process and work environment to attract and accomodate autistic individuals. Specifically, we'll touch upon:

  • Attracting applications for a role
  • The interview process
  • Accommodations within the workplace

Recruitment

Navigating the hiring process can be a daunting task for autistic individuals. Steps your company cam take to improve your hiring process include:

  • Directly approach candidates for roles. Evidence suggests austistic individuals apply for jobs at a much lower frequency than other cohorts (owing to a lack of confidence resulting from their under-representation in the workforce), but are equally as open to opportunities when approached
  • Clear and concise job adverts, plainly specifying role duties and responsibilities. Unnecessary detail and jargon may be overwhelming to some candidates
  • Be objective about the abilities and experiences that are essential for the job to be done well, and leave out any that are not
  • Working with autism- or neurodiverse-specialist recruiters
  • Specify that your company encourages applications for autistic individuals, alongside any workplace adjustments your company has made
